AccessMaps: Accessibility-Aware Mapping for Everyone

AccessMaps is a community-driven platform that helps people discover ramps, elevators, accessible restrooms, and other key features around them. We combine crowdsourced reports with a clean mobile interface to make everyday navigation more inclusive.

1. Project Overview

What we built

AccessMaps consists of an iOS app backed by a FastAPI service and relational database. Users can explore a map, view nearby accessible places, and submit new locations with photos and an accessibility checklist (ramp, elevator, restroom, parking, automatic doors).

Goal: put sidewalk-level accessibility details into the hands of the people who need them most, in real time.

2. Background & Motivation

Why this matters

For millions of people worldwide—wheelchair users, seniors, people with injuries, parents with strollers— route choice is about more than distance. A single missing curb cut or steep hill can make an otherwise “fast” route impossible.

However, detailed accessibility data is typically fragmented, outdated, or completely missing from standard navigation tools. City GIS teams may track some infrastructure, but conditions on the ground change faster than official datasets can be updated.

AccessMaps was motivated by:

  • A lack of accessibility-aware routing in mainstream map apps.
  • Growing interest in inclusive, human-centered urban design.
  • The potential of crowdsourcing to keep data fresh and localized.
Core Question: Can a community-driven mapping platform provide more relevant, sidewalk-level accessibility information than existing navigation systems?

3. System Design

High-level architecture

Our system follows a classic client–server model. The iOS app communicates with a FastAPI backend via JSON over HTTPS, backed by a PostgreSQL database hosted in the cloud.

  • Frontend: SwiftUI iOS app with MapKit.
  • API Layer: FastAPI with typed routes for places, auth, and profiles.
  • Data Storage: PostgreSQL for structured data, S3 for photos.
  • Auth: JWT-based login with email + password.

4. Core Features

What users can do
  • Explore accessible places on an interactive map.
  • View a list of nearby locations with key accessibility flags.
  • Submit new places with photos and a checklist of features.
  • Create a profile and manage account details.
  • Quickly search for specific places or categories (e.g., ramps, restrooms).

6. Evaluation & Future Work

What we learned & what’s next

Early testing with classmates and campus users showed strong interest in a tool focused specifically on accessibility, but also highlighted challenges: data completeness, verification, and incentives for ongoing contributions.

  • Improve onboarding and make the submission flow even faster.
  • Add moderation tools and a reputation system for trusted contributors.
  • Integrate routing (e.g., “step-free route from A to B”).
  • Partner with local disability resource centers for initial data seeding.
